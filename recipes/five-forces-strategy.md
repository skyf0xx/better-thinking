# Recipe: Five Forces Competitive Strategy

**What it is:** Michael Porter's framework for deciding whether a market is worth entering (or defending) by analyzing five structural pressures on profitability — competitive rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitutes, and threat of new entrants — rather than judging attractiveness from growth headlines alone.

## Skill sequence

1. **Map the actors** — run **[[stakeholder-mapping]]** to identify who actually occupies each force: direct competitors (rivalry), the suppliers you'd depend on, the buyers/customers and their concentration, substitute products or workarounds, and plausible new entrants. Most Five Forces exercises name the forces but skip naming the specific real actors behind each one.
2. **Analyze incentives per force** — run **[[incentive-analysis]]** on each mapped actor: what does a supplier gain or lose from your entry, what would make a buyer switch away from a substitute to you or vice versa, what would draw a new entrant in. This converts the five forces from a static checklist into an explicit model of who's incentivized to do what.
3. **Map the system** — run **[[systems-mapping]]** to connect the forces to each other, not just to the market in isolation — e.g. supplier power feeding buyer power (a concentrated supplier squeezing margins that then get passed to buyers), or substitute threat suppressing new-entrant appeal (a market entrants avoid because substitutes already cap the ceiling).
4. **Stress-test with scenarios** — run **[[scenario-planning]]** on how each force plausibly shifts over the relevant time horizon (a key supplier gets acquired, a substitute matures, a new entrant with more capital shows up) rather than analyzing the five forces as a frozen snapshot.
5. **Decide the strategic posture** — run **[[strategic-planning]]** to turn the force analysis and scenarios into an actual entry/defend/avoid decision and a resourced plan, not just a diagnostic report that stops at "the market is medium-attractive."
6. **Pressure-test the chosen posture** — run **[[game-theoretic-analysis]]** if competitors are likely to respond strategically to your move (pricing wars, retaliatory features) — Five Forces alone treats the other actors as static; this step checks whether they'd actually sit still.

## What this buys you over a plain Five Forces writeup

- Each force is grounded in named, specific actors via `stakeholder-mapping` instead of staying an abstract label ("supplier power: medium").
- Incentive analysis makes explicit *why* each actor would push in a given direction, instead of asserting force strength as a bare rating.
- Systems-mapping surfaces interactions between forces that a force-by-force checklist misses entirely.
- Scenario-planning treats the analysis as dynamic — forces change — rather than a one-time snapshot that goes stale the moment a supplier merger or new entrant appears.
- The output ends in an actual strategic decision and resourced plan, not a diagnostic five-paragraph memo that never converts to action.
- The optional game-theoretic pass catches the classic Five Forces blind spot: competitors who react to your entry rather than absorbing it passively.

## Residual gap

Five Forces is a market-structure lens, not a financial model — it doesn't produce revenue projections or unit economics. Pair with [[cost-benefit]] or [[expected-value]] if the entry decision needs an explicit financial threshold, not just a structural read on attractiveness.
