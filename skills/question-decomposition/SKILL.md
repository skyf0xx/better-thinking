# Question Decomposition

Break a research question into independently answerable sub-questions whose answers compose back into the original.

## Why

Big questions are unanswerable as posed, and research that starts without decomposing them first tends to wander. Sub-questions define what evidence would even count as an answer, and expose which specific part of the question is actually contested.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** starting any investigation; a question keeps producing vague, unsatisfying answers; scoping a literature search before diving in.
- **Don't use when:** the question is already atomic and doesn't decompose any further.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a research question.
- **Outputs:** a tree of sub-questions, each tagged by what kind of evidence would answer it, with the contested nodes marked explicitly.

## Principles

- Decompose until each leaf names its evidence type — a lookup, a measurement, a judgment call.
- Separate factual sub-questions from definitional and normative ones; they require genuinely different methods to resolve.
- The recomposition rule — how the sub-answers actually combine back into the original answer — must be stated, or the tree is just decoration.

## Procedure

1. Write the question and mark any ambiguous terms, pinning down their definitions.
2. Split along the question's natural joints: factual components, causal components, evaluative components.
3. Recurse until each leaf is answerable by a nameable method or source.
4. Tag each leaf: known, findable, genuinely open, or a value judgment.
5. State how the leaves recombine into a full answer — and identify which single leaf, depending on how it resolves, decides the whole thing (the crux).
6. Order the investigation: cruxes and cheap lookups first, and report any leaf that remains genuinely unresolved as the residual uncertainty.

## Common mistakes

- Decomposing along whatever data happens to be available rather than along the question's actual logical joints.
- Hiding a value judgment inside a leaf that looks factual on the surface.
- Never stating the recomposition rule, leaving the tree unable to actually answer the original question.

## Examples

- "Should we enter market X?" decomposed into market size, accessibility, competitive response, and our own capability fit.
- "Is this treatment effective?" decomposed into effective for whom, versus what alternative, on which outcome, over what time horizon.
