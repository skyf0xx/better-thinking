# Ethical Framework Rotation

Evaluate a decision or action through consequentialist, deontological, and virtue-ethics lenses in turn, and report where they agree and where they genuinely diverge.

## Why

Any single ethical framework has known blind spots — consequentialism can justify harmful means for good ends, deontology can produce rigid outcomes that ignore context, virtue ethics can be vague about specific action-guidance. Rotating through multiple frameworks surfaces disagreement a single-lens analysis would hide, and convergence across frameworks is much stronger evidence than any one framework's verdict alone.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** a decision has a genuine ethical dimension beyond pure preference or efficiency; a decision "feels" uncomfortable in a way cost-benefit alone doesn't capture; policy or precedent-setting decisions.
- **Don't use when:** the question is purely factual or preferential with no real ethical stakes.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a decision or action with an ethical dimension.
- **Outputs:** a verdict from each of three lenses, with explicit agreement or disagreement noted, and where they diverge, what's actually driving it.

## Principles

- Consequentialist: what outcome does this produce, for whom, weighed how?
- Deontological: does this respect rules, rights, and duties regardless of outcome — would it be acceptable if universalized?
- Virtue: what would a person of good character do here, and what does this action express about character?
- Divergence is not a tiebreak to resolve mechanically — it's the actual finding, worth sitting with rather than papering over.

## Procedure

1. State the decision or action precisely, including who's affected.
2. **Consequentialist pass:** enumerate outcomes and who bears them; weigh net welfare.
3. **Deontological pass:** identify duties, rights, and rules; ask if universalizable without contradiction or harm.
4. **Virtue pass:** ask what this action reveals about character, apart from outcome or rule.
5. Compare the three verdicts; agreement is robust, divergence names what's driving the split.
6. Report divergence honestly rather than picking the framework that gives the preferred answer.

## Common mistakes

- Running only the framework that happens to support a pre-existing preference.
- Treating disagreement between lenses as a bug to resolve by force rather than a genuine signal about the decision's difficulty.
- Skipping the exercise on decisions that "feel fine" — that's often exactly where a blind spot hides.

## Examples

- A business decision that's profitable but relies on a misleading disclosure.
- A policy that's dutifully rule-following but produces bad outcomes for a vulnerable group.
- A personal choice evaluated for what it says about the kind of person you're becoming.
