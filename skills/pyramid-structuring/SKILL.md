# Pyramid Structuring

Lead with the answer, then support it with grouped, logically ordered reasons — inverting the build-up-to-the-conclusion instinct.

## Why

Readers — especially busy, senior, or skimming ones — decide whether to keep reading in the first sentence. Burying the conclusion under build-up loses them before they reach it, and even attentive readers process supporting detail better once they already know what it's supporting.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** writing any document or making any case meant to inform a decision; a draft currently builds up to its point instead of leading with it.
- **Don't use when:** the piece is deliberately narrative or suspenseful — a different structure is the point there.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a conclusion and its supporting reasons or evidence.
- **Outputs:** a structure with the answer first, reasons grouped and ordered beneath it, each reason similarly structured recursively.

## Principles

- State the main point in the first sentence or two, not the last paragraph.
- Group supporting reasons so each group answers one implicit "why" or "how" question, not a random list.
- Order groups by importance to the reader's decision, not by the order you thought of them.
- Every level answers a question raised by the level above it — that's what makes it a pyramid rather than a list.

## Procedure

1. State the main conclusion or recommendation in one sentence.
2. List the reasons supporting it; group related reasons together, mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.
3. Order the groups by what matters most to the reader's decision.
4. Within each substantial group, apply the same structure recursively: sub-conclusion first, then its support.
5. Draft top-down — headline, then supporting structure, then details — never bottom-up.
6. Check: could a reader stop after the first paragraph and still know your point? If not, restructure; note any section that resisted this structure as a flag worth revisiting.

## Common mistakes

- Building suspense into an informational document ("first, some background…").
- Ordering support chronologically — how you learned it — instead of by importance to the reader.
- An "executive summary" that's actually just the introduction, not the conclusion.

## Examples

- A consulting memo's opening; a technical design doc's summary.
- An email requesting a decision, led with the ask.
- A legal brief's opening statement.
