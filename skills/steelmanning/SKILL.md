# Steelmanning

Reconstruct the strongest possible version of a position before evaluating or arguing against it.

## Why

Arguing against the weakest form of an opposing view — the strawman — feels like victory and teaches nothing. Steelmanning forces genuine contact with a position's actual force, which is where real disagreement, or real agreement, actually lives. Skipping it is how confident-sounding rebuttals collapse the moment someone states the position properly.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** about to argue against something; evaluating a proposal you're inclined to reject; writing a rebuttal to any position.
- **Don't use when:** the position is being evaluated purely for internal logical consistency — use a direct validity check instead, since steelmanning adds nothing there.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a position, argument, or person to disagree with.
- **Outputs:** the strongest coherent version of that position, in language its proponent would endorse, plus your actual point of departure from it.

## Principles

- If a proponent wouldn't recognize your summary as their view, you've built a strawman, not a steelman.
- Charity means supplying the best available premises and context the position's proponents actually have — not inventing an unrelated, more convenient argument for them.
- Steelmanning is not agreeing. It's earning the right to disagree by engaging with the real thing.

## Procedure

1. State the position as its weakest critics would — the strawman — for contrast.
2. Reconstruct it properly: with its best available evidence, most charitable premises, and the context that actually motivates it.
3. Check: would a thoughtful proponent say "yes, that's my view, stated well"? If not, revise until they would.
4. Identify what the steelmanned version gets right.
5. Locate your actual point of departure — the specific premise or piece of evidence where you diverge from it.
6. Respond to the steelman, not the strawman, and note explicitly if any part of the steelmanned position remains genuinely unresolved.

## Common mistakes

- Steelmanning into a different, more convenient position instead of the one actually held.
- Skipping straight to rebuttal without running the proponent-check in step 3.
- Steelmanning positions you already agree with — there's no work to do there; save the effort for positions you're inclined to resist.

## Examples

- Rebutting a competitor's technical claim by first stating their strongest possible case for it.
- Responding to a policy proposal you dislike by reconstructing its best justification before critiquing it.
- An internal design review steelmanning an approach the reviewer would have done differently, before raising concerns.
