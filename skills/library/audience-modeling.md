# Audience Modeling

Model the reader or listener's existing knowledge, goals, and likely objections before composing anything, and let that model drive every subsequent choice.

## Why

Content optimized for the writer's own understanding routinely fails the reader — too much unneeded context, too little of what they actually need, wrong things emphasized. Modeling the audience first fixes the target before aiming.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** before writing or presenting anything consequential; a draft isn't landing and it's unclear why.
- **Don't use when:** writing purely for yourself — notes, journaling.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a communication task plus what's known about the audience.
- **Outputs:** an audience model (knowledge, goal, likely objections, the decision they need to make) that subsequent structuring decisions can be checked against.

## Principles

- What the audience needs to know is not what you find interesting about the topic — it's what closes their specific gap.
- Assume less shared context than feels natural, but don't over-explain what they already know — both cost trust.
- The audience's objections are predictable if you model their incentives and prior position.
- Different audience members in one room may need different things — name the primary one.

## Procedure

1. Name the audience specifically — a role, not "readers in general."
2. State what they already know and don't know about this topic.
3. State what decision or action they need to take after receiving this — that's the actual goal, not "inform them."
4. Predict their top 1–3 objections or points of skepticism.
5. Note what they care about that you might not — their incentives, their constraints.
6. Let this model drive structure, depth, and word choice in the actual composition; flag any part of the model that's a guess rather than known.

## Common mistakes

- Modeling the audience once, generically, and reusing it unchanged for every piece.
- Writing to impress rather than to close the audience's specific gap.
- Assuming your own level of context is universal.

## Examples

- An engineering design doc written for a non-technical stakeholder versus a peer reviewer.
- A sales pitch adapted per buyer persona.
- A doctor explaining a diagnosis to a patient versus to a colleague.
