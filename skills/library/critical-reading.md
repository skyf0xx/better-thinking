# Critical Reading

Extract from a text its claims, evidence, assumptions, and omissions — reading what it does, not just what it says.

## Why

Texts are optimized to persuade, and passive reading absorbs the author's frame along with their facts. Structured extraction separates what was *demonstrated* from what was *asserted* from what was *implied by omission* — three very different epistemic statuses fluent prose blurs together.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** any consequential document, especially polished, confident, or agreeable prose — agreement is exactly when critical reading is hardest and most needed.
- **Don't use when:** reading purely for orientation — skim first, read critically what turns out to matter.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a document — paper, memo, article, contract, transcript.
- **Outputs:** a claims inventory tagged by support level, the argument's skeleton, its assumptions, and its notable silences.

## Principles

- Identify the conclusion first, then audit backwards — texts bury weak links in the middle.
- Distinguish demonstrated, cited, asserted, and insinuated claims as separate categories.
- What's missing is itself a claim — the comparison never made, the denominator never given.
- Note the text's frame before it sets your own.

## Procedure

1. Skim for structure: what is this text trying to get the reader to believe or do?
2. Extract the main claims verbatim — early paraphrasing hides sleight of hand.
3. Tag each claim's support: demonstrated here, cited (check what the citation actually says), asserted, or implied.
4. Surface the assumptions the argument needs but never states.
5. Hunt omissions: what would a knowledgeable skeptic expect this text to address, and where is its silence?
6. Separate verdicts: what did this text actually *establish* versus merely *say* — report anything unresolved as residual uncertainty.

## Common mistakes

- Critique-by-vibes — disliking a conclusion and treating that as an audit of its support.
- Trusting citations without opening them; they routinely don't say what they're cited for.
- Reading hostile texts critically while reading friendly ones passively.

## Examples

- A vendor whitepaper making a competitive claim.
- A policy brief, an earnings call transcript, or a paper's methods section.
- Rereading your own draft a day later with the same scrutiny you'd apply to someone else's.
