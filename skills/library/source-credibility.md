# Source Credibility

Grade a source on expertise, incentive, track record, and proximity to the events before letting its claims carry weight.

## Why

Content persuades through fluency, but credibility lives in metadata — who is saying it, how they'd know, and what they gain from being believed. Grading the source first prevents fluent nonsense from outranking awkward, well-supported truth.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** weighing any consequential claim; sources conflict; a claim is surprising or unusually convenient.
- **Don't use when:** the claim is directly checkable — go check it instead of grading the messenger.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a source making a claim.
- **Outputs:** a credibility grade, with the specific caveats to apply going forward.

## Principles

- Proximity beats prestige — a direct eyewitness outranks a famous but distant commentator.
- Expertise is domain-specific and decays sharply at the domain boundary.
- Incentive doesn't disprove a claim, it discounts it — ask what the source gains if believed.
- A track record of *corrected* errors is a positive signal, not a negative one.

## Procedure

1. Identify the actual origin of the claim versus whoever is merely repeating it.
2. Assess proximity: how many hops removed from the events is this source?
3. Assess competence: does the source have real expertise in *this* domain?
4. Assess incentive: what does the source gain if believed? Flag, don't auto-dismiss.
5. Assess track record: prior accuracy, correction behavior, and any report against their own interest — a strong positive signal.
6. Grade: rely, use with caveats, corroborate first, or discount — attach the specific caveat as the residual uncertainty.

## Common mistakes

- Halo transfer — trusting an expert in domain A when they speak about unrelated domain B.
- The genetic fallacy — dismissing a true claim for coming from a biased source; bias should discount weight, not flip truth.
- Letting prestige substitute for actual proximity.
- Ignoring statements against the source's own interest, among the most underrated evidence available.

## Examples

- A vendor's own benchmark results versus an independent customer's account.
- An anonymous insider's claim versus an official public statement.
- A celebrated pundit's take versus a comparatively boring domain specialist's.
