# Problem Framing

Restate a problem several distinct ways before accepting any framing, because the framing chooses the solution space.

## Why

Most bad solutions are good solutions to the wrong problem. The initial framing arrives already loaded with assumptions about cause, scope, and solution shape, and generating genuine rival framings is the only reliable defense against inheriting them unexamined.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** starting any nontrivial problem; proposed solutions all feel wrong despite being individually reasonable; someone hands you a solution disguised as a problem ("we need a dashboard").
- **Don't use when:** the problem is routine and its framing is already settled by precedent that's actually still valid.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a problem as first stated.
- **Outputs:** three or more alternative framings, the chosen framing with its justification, and what each rejected framing would have optimized for instead.

## Principles

- A "problem" is a gap between a current state and a standard — vary both, not just the state.
- The presenting problem is often a symptom, a constraint, or someone's already-preferred solution in disguise.
- Who owns the problem changes what the problem actually is.

## Procedure

1. Write the problem exactly as given, verbatim.
2. Identify the assumptions embedded in that statement: a presumed cause, a presumed solution shape, a presumed owner, a presumed constraint.
3. Generate rival framings: zoom out (what larger goal does this serve?), zoom in (which sub-part actually hurts?), invert the actor (whose problem is this really?), question the standard (is the expectation wrong rather than the state?).
4. For each framing, note what a solution would concretely look like — genuinely different solution shapes confirm the framings are truly distinct, not cosmetic variations.
5. Choose the framing that best matches the underlying goal, and record why.
6. State what evidence would indicate this chosen framing was actually wrong — that's the residual uncertainty to carry forward.

## Common mistakes

- Framing-shopping until landing on one that happens to yield the answer already wanted.
- Infinite reframing used as a form of procrastination rather than genuine exploration.
- Accepting the requester's framing uncritically just because they're senior or the framing arrived confidently.

## Examples

- "The elevator is too slow" reframed as a boredom problem (solved with mirrors) versus a throughput problem (solved with more elevators).
- "We need more engineers" reframed as a scope problem versus a process problem.
- "Users can't find the button" reframed by asking whether they even need that action in the first place.
