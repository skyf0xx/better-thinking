# Interest-Based Bargaining

Negotiate on the underlying interests behind each side's stated positions, rather than splitting the difference between the positions themselves.

## Why

Positions are often incompatible even when the underlying interests aren't — two parties can both get what they actually need through a solution neither stated position anticipated. Bargaining on positions alone leaves this value on the table.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** a negotiation has stalled on incompatible positions; both sides seem to be optimizing for the wrong variable.
- **Don't use when:** the negotiation is genuinely zero-sum on a single dimension — then it's just BATNA analysis and leverage.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** a negotiation where stated positions conflict.
- **Outputs:** the interests underlying each position, and options that satisfy both sides' interests better than any position-splitting compromise.

## Principles

- Ask "why do you want that?" of every stated position — the answer is the interest.
- Interests are often compatible even when positions aren't.
- Generate options before committing to a position — invent multiple ways to satisfy both sides' interests, then select.
- Separate the people from the problem — attack the problem jointly, not each other.

## Procedure

1. State each side's stated position explicitly.
2. For each, ask why, repeatedly if needed, until reaching the underlying interest — security, recognition, autonomy, resource need, timeline.
3. Check for compatible or complementary interests hidden behind incompatible positions.
4. Generate multiple options that could satisfy the interests, before evaluating any.
5. Evaluate options jointly against both sides' interests, using objective criteria where possible rather than raw leverage.
6. Select and formalize the option that best satisfies both sides' actual interests.

## Common mistakes

- Stopping at the first "why" instead of digging to the real interest.
- Treating interest-based bargaining as a way to disguise a fixed position rather than genuinely opening to new options.
- Skipping straight to a proposed solution before both sides' interests are actually understood.

## Examples

- Two departments fighting over budget where the real interests are predictability and flexibility.
- A labor negotiation where "wage increase" masks an interest in fairness or recognition.
- A custody arrangement negotiation.
