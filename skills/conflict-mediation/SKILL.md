# Conflict Mediation

As a third party, surface each side's narrative and underlying interests, find compatible ground, and broker an agreement neither side could reach alone.

## Why

Parties in conflict are often too invested in their own narrative to hear the other's, and too anchored on positions to see compatible interests. A mediator's structural advantage is exactly this outside position — used well, it does what direct negotiation between the parties couldn't.

## Use when / Don't use when

- **Use when:** direct negotiation between parties has stalled or is too charged for them to conduct alone; a neutral structure is needed to make progress.
- **Don't use when:** the parties can and should resolve it directly — mediation shouldn't substitute for direct communication capability they actually have.

## Inputs → Outputs

- **Inputs:** two or more parties in unresolved conflict plus a mediator role.
- **Outputs:** a mediated agreement or, at minimum, mutual understanding of the other's position and a defined next step.

## Principles

- The mediator holds the process, not the outcome — pushing a preferred resolution undermines the role's legitimacy.
- Each side needs to feel genuinely heard by the mediator before they can hear the other side.
- Mediator-translated steelmanning of each side's position often lands better than the original party's own framing.
- The goal may be agreement, or may just be de-escalation and a defined next step — both are legitimate.

## Procedure

1. Meet with each side separately first if the conflict is heated; use `active-listening` fully.
2. Run `empathy-mapping` on each side to understand their actual situation, not just their stated complaint.
3. Identify each side's underlying interests via `interest-based-bargaining` probing, done separately if needed.
4. Bring sides together when ready; restate each side's position using `steelmanning` and `nonviolent-communication` structure to lower defensiveness.
5. Guide toward compatible interests found in step 3; facilitate joint option generation rather than proposing your own solution.
6. If agreement isn't reachable, secure a defined smaller next step rather than forcing false resolution.

## Common mistakes

- The mediator taking a side or pushing their own preferred solution.
- Skipping separate initial listening and going straight to a joint session while the conflict is still hot.
- Declaring failure when no full agreement is reached instead of securing the achievable partial outcome.

## Examples

- A manager mediating between two conflicting team members.
- A family mediation.
- Cross-team conflict over resourcing.
